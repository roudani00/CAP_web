Tank Water Evaporative Cooling

So what's the problem?

In Kuwait, rooftop water storage tanks are highly susceptible to extreme heating due to intense solar radiation and ambient temperatures often exceeding 45–50 °C during summer months. This exposure causes stored water temperatures to rise above 50 °C, making it uncomfortable for domestic use such as showers or drinking and increasing the risk of bacterial growth in stagnant warm water. Local efforts like insulation wraps, shading canopies, or reflective paints provide limited relief, with typical tanks losing efficiency from nighttime heat loss or structural wear. Advanced solutions, such as earth-water heat exchangers buried 5 m deep, can temper water to below 33 °C by leveraging cooler sub-soil temperatures, highlighting the need for innovative cooling to mitigate energy-intensive alternatives like electric chillers.

TWEC introduces an innovative "tank-in-a-tank" design to enhance rooftop water cooling in Kuwait's harsh climate. The inner tank holds the potable water, while the outer tank creates an annular space for evaporative cooling. A motor-driven fan induces air flow through this space, and a misting system periodically releases fine water droplets onto the inner tank's exterior surface. As the mist evaporates due to the forced air circulation, it absorbs heat from the inner tank walls, effectively cooling the stored water without direct mixing or contamination. This passive-assisted approach minimizes energy use compared to traditional chillers, with sensors monitoring temperature and humidity to optimize misting intervals

The solution

What's evaporative cooling?

Evaporative cooling operates on the principle of water evaporation absorbing latent heat from the surrounding air or surfaces, thereby lowering temperatures without mechanical refrigeration. When water is misted or spread over a surface, it transitions from liquid to vapor, drawing thermal energy from the environment and creating a cooling effect proportional to the evaporation rate. This process is most effective in low-humidity, high-temperature climates like Kuwait, where dry air enhances evaporation efficiency. Factors such as air flow (via fans) and surface area increase the rate, but performance drops in high humidity as saturated air limits further evaporation. Commonly used in swamp coolers or cooling towers, it offers energy savings over compressor-based systems but requires water replenishment.

Saving energy in water cooling systems is crucial in Kuwait, where high electricity demand from desalination and air conditioning strains the grid, contributing to environmental challenges like CO2 emissions from fossil fuel reliance. Efficient designs like evaporative cooling reduce compressor runtime, lowering power consumption by up to 20–30% compared to conventional methods, while integrating renewables such as solar panels further promotes sustainability. Amid rising population and urbanization, energy conservation mitigates water scarcity issues and supports compliance with international climate agreements, fostering long-term economic and ecological benefits.
